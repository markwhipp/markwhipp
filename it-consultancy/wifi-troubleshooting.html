Trouble Shooting Wi-Fi and Network Issues for Restaurants

Written by Mark Whipp
on 13/01/2020

Whilst fully troubleshooting all network faults is beyond most hostpitality managers skillset there is a fair bit that they can do all the same before calling in the professionals. A great deal of network troubleshooting is simply a matter of following basic logic. The most common problems I am called in to test and check relate to network connectivity. If you are happy to always call your IT support then that is fine. If, however, you prefer to see if you can work out what is wrong yourself then I am going to try and give you some simple pointers on how to do this. These pointers might also help you in an emergency, when your IT supplier is unavailable.

One of the biggest problems I encounter when restaurant managements teams call me for support is that they lump all elements of connectivity together. Failing to separate the individual steps makes it hard for them to work out where the problem is exactly. Where they should concentrate their checking. A very common statement I hear is "my Wi-Fi is not working". This generally means one or more of their Wi-Fi devices is not connecting. In the following paragraphs, I will attempt to explain a simple way to test and diagnose this issue.

Even before starting to diagnose the connectivity chain of your network communications, the first thing to check is any other similar devices. Is it just one device not working or is it all of them. If it is only one wireless device, it is likely (logical) that the problem relates to that device itself. You should troubleshoot that device and not worry too much about the network. If, on the other hand, all wireless devices are not working, then the next thing you should check is if any of the Ethernet cable attached devices are working. If they are, then the WiFi systems are probably at fault. If they are not, then it is more likely that the actual Internet circuit is the problem.

Knowing the route that the network traffic takes can make this easier to see why. Your device first needs to get onto the restaurant network. Cable attached devices have a slightly simpler route. The traffic runs down the cable to the network switch. It then goes from the switch to the boundary router via an uplink cable from the network switch. The boundary router, if working, then send this traffic to the Internet Server that your testing device is requesting. If both the Ethernet and WiFi devices are not getting there traffic out, the common denominator is the boundary router. Generally the simplest test here is to reboot the router. You might also whant to test for a dial tone on the analogue line of the broadband dongle?? if you are using a DSL circuit. Failing this, you will need to contact your IT support or Internet Service Provider for assistance. This is a problem with your Internet circuit and not your WiFi.

It could well be that your cable attached devices are working but your Wifi devices are not. This is a Wi-Fi problem. This would indicate an issue with a Wireless Access Point. Your wireless devices traffic generally goes via the same network switch, uplink cable and boundary router as the cable attached devices. We know these devices are working correctly as the cable attached devices are working. However, before getting to the switch, the Wi-Fi attached devices must first go through a Wireless Access Point to get there.

At this point, you may want to check the Wireless Access Point. Does it have a light on indicating that it is powered up correctly? Are all the cables connected to the switch correctly. Do they have a link light on the switch? It is now probably time to call your IT support personnel. You will at least be able to tell them what you have done. What you have checked and where you think the issue is. Hopefully this will make it even easier for them to troubleshoot and fix your issue.
